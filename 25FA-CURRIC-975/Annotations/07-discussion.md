As a massive fan of makerspaces (even though I'm still learning to be more present in those spaces), I was really attached to the paper on the indigenous making paper for multiple reasons. One big one was that I had previously worked on a team looking to build cultural lanterns into engineering kits so we had explored trying to make a more multi-cultural audience to learn and teach about the different perspectives each culture has with their relation to light and lanterns. What we were trying to do back then was to build a cultural artifact with modern tools and technologies in an effort to modernize the tradition. Looking back we had a very artifact-centric approach. We focused on the product of the lantern and not the practices around assembly and leading up to the tradition. This sadly makes sense since according to the paper, this "materiality [is] reflective of and linked to the origins of settler-colonlialism" (F. Barajas-Lopez and M. Bang).

However, what from what is pitched in the Claywork paper was a flip on our approach. Making the embodied practices of walking, storytelling, and then finally making the emphasis rather than the end product. In some ways I'm reminded then of yearbooks and how their value isn't in the product, but rather as a product of the shared experiences of a school and class. In the same sense then, the product of claymaking is representative of it's process and stories as opposed to being a means to a product.
