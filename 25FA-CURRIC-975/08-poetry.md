## Poetry styles

### Conceptual poetry

> a poem that is self-creating and combines unlikely topics by nature

- appropriation: taking existing text and re-presenting it as a poem
- constraint-based writing / structured generation: using strict non-meaning-driven rule to generate the text
	- ie. Oulipian Constraints: n+7 where n is replaced by a noun following the original in a dictionary
- found poetry: poem selecting different parts from other sources
- associative leap
	- free verse jumping between different types of topics

Structured Generation Poetry
### Oulipian Constraints

> using strict non-meaning-driven rule to generate the text

| **Method**             | **Constraint/Procedure**                                                                                                                              | **Generative Effect**                                                                                                                                                         |
| ---------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Lipogram**           | Exclude one or more common letters (often the letter 'e') from the entire poem.                                                                       | Forces an extreme restructuring of vocabulary, resulting in unusual and inventive syntax. Georges Perec's novel _A Void_ is the most famous example.                          |
| **Univocalism**        | The entire poem may only use words containing a single vowel (e.g., only 'A' words).                                                                  | Drastically limits the available lexicon, creating a text with highly constrained, often musical, sound and strange word combinations.                                        |
| **Snowball**           | Each line of the poem consists of a single word, and each successive word is one letter longer than the word before it.                               | A visual and structural constraint that drives word choice purely by letter count, leading to often-humorous or nonsensical development.                                      |
| **Perverb**            | Take a familiar proverb (e.g., "A bird in the hand is worth two in the bush") and substitute its words with parts of other proverbs.                  | Generates a new, bizarre, and sometimes profound "anti-proverb" by scrambling cultural clichés.                                                                               |
| **Sestina/Villanelle** | While a traditional form, these are based on _repetition constraints_. Specific words or lines must be repeated in a predetermined, rotating pattern. | The fixed pattern of repetition forces the content of the poem to evolve and deepen around a handful of anchor words/lines, generating complex emotional or narrative layers. |

### Chance operations

> Dadist origination. uses randomness to bypass author's ego and intent.

| **Method**            | **Constraint/Procedure**                                                                                                                                                                                    | **Generative Effect**                                                                                                                                                                       |
| --------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Cut-Up Technique**  | Take a text (e.g., a newspaper article or book page), cut it into individual words or phrases, place them in a bag, and pull them out one-by-one to create the poem.                                        | A purely mechanical process that creates jarring and random juxtapositions of language, leading to startling, unintended meanings. (Famously used by William S. Burroughs and Kurt Cobain.) |
| **Chance Operations** | Using a random input (like a coin flip, dice roll, or the _I Ching_) to determine a compositional choice, such as the number of words, the selection of vocabulary from a list, or the ordering of stanzas. | Ensures the resulting poem is a product of non-human will, often revealing unexpected connections in the source material. (Pioneered by John Cage and Jackson Mac Low.)                     |
### Appropriation and algorithmic generation (found content)

> use existing language or data as the generative "engine" for the poem, making the concept of the procedure the primary artistic act.

| **Method**                            | **Constraint/Procedure**                                                                                                                                                                                  | **Generative Effect**                                                                                                                                                  |
| ------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Found Poetry / Erasure Poetry**     | Select an existing text (like a document, government report, or book page) and either **frame** it as a poem (Found) or **erase** most of the words to reveal a new poem in the remaining text (Erasure). | The constraints of the source document's language and layout force the poet to find unexpected, hidden poetry within non-poetic material.                              |
| **Flarf (Google Sculpting)**          | Use highly unusual, often absurd or cloying, phrases as search terms on the Internet, then collect and collage the resulting snippets from the first few pages of search results.                         | Generates a poem that reflects the chaotic, contradictory, and often low-culture "junk-speech" of the public internet, often with unexpected humor or social critique. |
| **Markov Chain / Algorithmic Poetry** | Using a computer program to analyze the vocabulary and word patterns (syntax) of a source text, then generate new text that mimics the source's style without retaining its meaning.                      | The algorithm acts as the constraint, generating a new poem that sounds familiar but is filled with strange, generative new phrases and combinations.                  |
