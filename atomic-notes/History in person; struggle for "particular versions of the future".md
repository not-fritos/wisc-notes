> > As the young women constructed selves and considered possible futures, their efforts were viewed as a struggle or fight “for particular versions of the future” (Holland & Lave, 2001, p. 28) or a future self—within and against the institutional arrangements and discourses they faced. 1 For example, as these students worked to construct science, engineering, or math identities in school, they had to do so in the presence of robust and enduring national narratives and institutional arrangements that were realized in local contexts of practice. And thus, similar to other identity work, these self-authorings were continually contested (or struggled over) in local practice (Calabrese Barton et al., 2012; Davidson, 1996; Holland, Lachicotte, Skinner, & Cain, 1998; Holland & Lave, 2001; Nasir & Saxe, 2003). Following Holland and Lave (2001), struggles, or more accurately struggling, can refer to (a) engagements with enduring conflicts at an institutional, political, “capital S” level (e.g., persistent disputes about the meaning of achievement or equity in the U.S. education system) or (b) engagements with local conflicts that arise in day-to-day practices (e.g., contests over status and prestige within a classroom group; p. 21). Dealing with these struggles is how history comes to be lived in persons.
