> [!PDF|note] [[Allen & Eisenhart (2017).pdf#page=17&selection=45,0,70,8&color=note|Allen & Eisenhart (2017), p.17]]
> > Katie’s comments here suggest an apparent disconnect between the discourse about Black students and their actual advanced course–taking practices. That is, the numbers that Katie provided suggest (if the numbers are correct) 4 that the percentage of Black students taking advanced courses at the school was actually quite high. When one accounts for the percentage of Black students in the class of 600, roughly 52% of these students were in AP courses and 83% in honors. Although these numbers, if true, are impressive and an indicator of the access students of color had to advanced courses at Capital, Katie’s comment also suggests how, despite the numbers, the broader historical narrative at the school positioned White students favorably while suggesting that Black students had lower academic goals and ambitions. Of course, it is likely that Katie’s acceptance of the Black Student Union statement was supported by her experience as one of the very few Blacks compared to Whites in her advanced courses at Capital.
