> [!PDF|note] [[Allen & Eisenhart (2017).pdf#page=26&selection=39,15,55,51&color=note|Allen & Eisenhart (2017), p.26]]
> > Rather, their struggle was with racial and class discourses at school that positioned them differently than they were or wished to be. The women’s success in school, particularly in math, and their consequent positioning (qualifications, interests, identities) to pursue STEM were byproducts of their struggles to deal with local narratives—derived from larger enduring historical conflicts—that threatened to undermine their status and compromise their futures because of their race and class. This is how the history of race and class privilege was being lived and navigated by these young women.

> [!PDF|note] [[Allen & Eisenhart (2017).pdf#page=27&selection=4,46,7,68&color=note|Allen & Eisenhart (2017), p.27]]
> > The imperative for the school to fix the achievement gap was pronounced, but the onus was primarily on the students of color to step up to opportunities provided at school rather than on the school to consider how its practices might better serve its students of color.

> > At Southside, with its homogeneous population, the achievement gap was not a part of the local discourse. Rather, the narrative was about creating opportunities to bring students up from their unfortunate social circumstances as impoverished Mexican American immigrant youth. Latino/a students were described as collectively behind academically but through no fault of their own.
