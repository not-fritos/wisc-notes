> > Furthermore, schooling practices can make it seem that science is the province of people who are privileged in society—upper middle class, White, and male (Bang, Warren, Rosebery, & Medin, 2013; Calabrese Barton et al., 2012; Carlone & Johnson, 2007; Carlone, Scott, & Lowder, 2014). School science is often treated as neutral with respect to cultural and social experiences (Bang et al., 2013), and instruction tends to focus on achieving established answers in uniform ways (Calabrese Barton et al., 2012; Carlone et al., 2014). Such practices reinforce the notion that science is for an elite few and work to shape students’ views of who or what is scientific and whether they fit such depictions. As young women of color engage in local practices, they must wrestle with intersecting social markers, such as race, class, and gender (Bruning, Bystydzienski, & Eisenhart, 2015; Foor & Walden, 2009; Hancock, 2007; Smooth, 2010), that often do not align with the imagery of science or technology. Furthermore, some normative ways of being (e.g., heteronormative forms of female gender performance) may usurp performances of other desired selves (e.g., being scientific; Carlone, Johnson, & Scott, 2015).
