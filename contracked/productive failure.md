- [[Productive failure refers to how ill-structured problems can still be productive]]

*This study investigates 11th grade science students and found that students initially struggled more with ill-structured problems but performed better on subsequent measures that were well-structured.*

- Types of structure
	- problem or task
	- scaffolding
	- providing tools and resources
	- expert help
- "what can be accomplished"
	- beyond current ability of learner
	- complex problem

## Motivations for exploring friction

- [[Students that had delayed structure, after they had attempted to solve the problem themselves, had higher gains in learning.]]
- [[Inventing to prepare for learning (IPL) was collaborative, but was more interested in individual learning]]

## Structure and CSCL

- [[CSCL research doesn't look at unstructured learning at all]]
- [[CSCL currently explores many types of structures]]
- [[Scaffolding helps learners through ZPD]]
- [[Current research doesn't look at the value of learning with minimal scaffolding because (1) assumes learning is entirely observable (2) how you measure makes it easy to validate previous studies (3) "lock-in" to a specific strategy may reduce transfer]]
- [[Tradeoffs of not locking into a strategy is that learners may not succeed. This is risky.]]
- [[Transfer requires studying similarities and differences.]]
- [[Can failure be productive and can we find this with contrasting-case design?]]

## Research design

- 7 schools
- 103 groups (50 IS ill structured / 51 WS well structured)
- Post-test 1: 2 WS problem scenarios without any of the extra information from the IS problem-scenarios. This measured near-transfer.
- Post-test 2: 2 IS problems required more advanced concepts in addition to previous concepts that are different from previous IS concepts. This measured far transfer within the same domain

	- elaborate story problem similar to a court case physics problem

	- greater number of interactions between parameters
		- no correct answer here, but requires argumentative reasoning
- [[Quantitative Content Analysis is used to segment and code utterances.]]
- [[Results found that IS groups focused more on problem analysis, problem critique, then criteria development.]]
- [[Results found that WS groups spent more time on solution development and solution evaluation.]]
- [[lag-sequential analysis (LSA) chunks up interactions by finding statistically significant transitions between activities. ]]
- [[ill-structured dynamics have a greater complexity in the sequential patterns of discussions.]]

- [[Kauffmans' (1995) measure of complexity for the evolution of Boolean networks]]
- [[Clear problem definitions skips critiquing problem analysis.]]
- [[IS problems led to many attempts at problem analysis followed with critique of analysis.]]
- [[Convergence was used as a second measure for complexity because the two measures used were new.]]
- [[Convergence is how group members interact to develop a consensus in problem-solving. Lower complexity means higher likelihood of convergence.]]
- [[IS groups had greater complexity and less convergence.]]
- [[Group performance was measured using a rubric since there were no objectively correct answers. IS students scored lower on group solution because they spent more time in the `analysis <-> critique` loop.]]
