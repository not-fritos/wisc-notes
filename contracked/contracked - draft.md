# Contracked: Collaborative Productive Failure with Minimal Scaffolding in Modular Synthesis
#project #paper

authors

This paper introduces Contracked, a project to discover the limits of productive failure and what the minimal amount of scaffolding or scripting is to lead to continued engagement in a collaborative, frustrating, open-ended problem-space. Many TEI learning environments focus on bounded learning spaces in which learners would be expected to get things right or complete the lesson; many learning experiences in everyday life do not conform to this. In this project, we are testing the lower bounds for scaffolding in a purposefully complicated, complex, and frustrating creative musical learning space, known as a modular synth. The modular synth has multiple affordances for tangible learning and experimentation: it is physical, it coheres to a unix-like “pipe” system of modularity, it makes immediate musical noise based on the interconnections of simple elements, it feels fun and novel to subjects, and it is robust enough to be used widely in live performance. It has two frictional features: it is expensive and confusing. This paper describes a design for scaffolding and a set of experiments with those designs in order to better understand both how to design scaffolds for confusing, complex, frustrating learning experiences and how to understand how people collaborate in those experiences.

### Modular Synths
- 3 sentence paragraph of history
- Longer paragraph about related work in TEI (also CHI, ISLS)
- 3 sentences on how they work - control voltage, 1v/oct, and “sound”
- 2 sentences on why a good model to answer this question

Modular synthesizers are instruments that are capable of creating electronic music using modular parts to create custom signal paths. They were simultaneously developed independently by two musicians, Don Buchla and Robert Moog and transformed the music scene starting in the 1960s. The modular nature of independent components enabled incredible degrees of creative freedom and presented an emphasis on building "a system or a set of rules which, once set in motion, will create music for you."
